"I want to make six figures this year." It's one of the most common freelance goals — and one of the least useful ways to frame what you actually want. $100,000 in gross revenue sounds compelling until you subtract taxes, business expenses, and the months when work dried up. What's left might be far less than $100,000 suggests.

Setting a **freelance revenue goal** that actually works means working backward from what you need and want your life to look like — not forward from a round number that sounds impressive. Done right, a revenue goal becomes a daily decision-making tool: which clients to take, what to charge, when to say no, and how much runway you have.

This guide walks you through the complete framework for building a revenue goal that's specific, realistic, and actually tied to the life you want to live.

---

## Why Round Number Goals Fail Freelancers

Most freelancers set revenue goals the way people set New Year's resolutions: aspirational, round, and disconnected from any underlying logic. $100K. $150K. $200K. These numbers feel meaningful, but without the math behind them, they're just wishes.

The problem with round number goals:

**They ignore the tax gap.** A freelancer earning $100,000 in gross revenue in the US can expect to pay 25–35% in combined federal, state, and self-employment taxes. That leaves $65,000–$75,000 — which after business expenses might be $55,000–$65,000 in actual take-home. That's very different from $100K.

**They don't account for slow months.** Freelance income is lumpy. A $120,000 annual goal averages $10,000/month — but in reality, some months might be $4,000 and others $18,000. A revenue goal that doesn't address this volatility doesn't help you manage it.

**They don't connect to actual decisions.** "I want to make $150K" doesn't tell you whether to take a $3,000 project, whether to raise your rates, or whether you can afford to hire a subcontractor. A goal built from real numbers does.

## The Framework: Building Your Revenue Goal From the Bottom Up

Instead of starting with a top-line revenue number, build your goal from the ground up — starting with what you actually need.

### Step 1: Define Your Personal Income Requirement

Start with your personal monthly expenses — not your dream expenses, but your actual ones:

- Housing (rent or mortgage, renter's/homeowner's insurance)
- Groceries and household supplies
- Transportation (car payment, insurance, fuel or transit)
- Utilities, internet, phone
- Health insurance and out-of-pocket medical
- Debt payments (student loans, credit cards, personal loans)
- Subscriptions and memberships (personal, not business)
- Dining out and personal entertainment
- Clothing and personal care
- Travel and vacations (amortize annual costs by 12)

Add a savings target — at minimum, you should be building an emergency fund (aim for 3–6 months of expenses) and contributing to retirement. The [Bureau of Labor Statistics Consumer Expenditure Survey](https://www.bls.gov/cex/) shows that most Americans significantly underestimate their actual monthly spending when asked to guess.

**Example personal monthly requirement: $5,200/month = $62,400/year**

### Step 2: Add Business Expenses

Your revenue goal needs to cover not just your personal income but the cost of running the business that generates that income. Common freelance business expenses:

- Software subscriptions: $100–$500/month
- Equipment (amortized): $50–$150/month
- Professional development: $50–$200/month
- Marketing and portfolio: $50–$150/month
- Accounting and legal: $50–$200/month
- Office or coworking: $0–$500/month
- Business insurance: $50–$200/month

**Example business expenses: $800/month = $9,600/year**

### Step 3: Calculate Your Tax Reserve

Self-employed freelancers pay self-employment tax (15.3% on net self-employment income in the US) plus federal and state income taxes. Depending on your situation, 25–35% of your net self-employment income may go to taxes.

Calculate your tax reserve based on your net income (personal income target), not gross revenue:

- Personal income target: $62,400/year
- 30% tax reserve: $62,400 × 0.30 = $18,720/year

**Example tax reserve: $18,720/year = $1,560/month**

### Step 4: Sum Your Annual Revenue Requirement

Add the three components:

| Component | Annual |
|-----------|--------|
| Personal income | $62,400 |
| Business expenses | $9,600 |
| Tax reserve | $18,720 |
| **Minimum Revenue Requirement** | **$90,720** |

This is your **break-even revenue goal** — the minimum you need to cover everything. Use the [Break-Even Calculator](/tools/break-even-calculator) to generate this number for your specific situation.

### Step 5: Add a Profit and Growth Margin

Break-even isn't a goal — it's a floor. Your actual revenue goal should include a buffer above break-even for:

- Unexpected expenses (equipment failure, medical bills, business surprises)
- Faster savings growth (above baseline)
- Business investment (courses, tools, subcontractors, marketing)
- Slow month protection (buffer against months that come in below average)

A practical profit margin above break-even: **20–30%**

$90,720 × 1.25 = **$113,400 annual revenue goal**

Now $100K actually has meaning: it's not quite enough to hit your profit target, but it covers your floor.

## Translating Revenue Goals Into Actionable Metrics

An annual revenue number sitting in a document does nothing. To be useful, it needs to translate into daily and weekly actions.

### Monthly Revenue Target

$113,400 ÷ 12 = **$9,450/month average**

But account for the lumpiness of freelance income. Rather than treating every month as equal, build a monthly model:

- High months (well-booked, multi-project): target $12,000–$15,000
- Average months: target $9,000–$11,000
- Slow months (expected 2–3 per year): may come in at $4,000–$7,000

The high months should carry the slow months. If you expect 3 slow months averaging $5,000 (total $15,000), the remaining 9 months need to average ~$10,900 to hit $113,400.

### Billable Hours Required

If your current rate is $125/hour:

$9,450/month ÷ $125/hour = **75.6 billable hours/month**

That's about 17–18 hours per week of billable work. Achievable — but you need to track whether you're actually hitting it. Many freelancers are surprised by how few hours they bill when they actually count.

Use the [Revenue Goal Calculator](/tools/revenue-goal-calculator) to run these numbers for your rate and target income.

### Client Count and Project Size

Another way to break down your revenue goal: how many clients or projects do you need?

If your average project is $4,500:
- $9,450/month ÷ $4,500/project = **2.1 projects/month** on average

If you work with retainer clients at $2,500/month:
- $9,450 ÷ $2,500 = **3.8 retainer clients** to hit your goal

These numbers immediately surface what's realistic. Three to four retainer clients is very doable. Two to three projects per month from new clients is harder — it requires a consistent pipeline.

## Adjusting for Freelance Income Volatility

One of the most important aspects of a freelance revenue goal that most guides miss: **how to manage the gap between average and actual.**

The solution is a cash buffer — and building it into your revenue goal planning.

**Target cash reserve:** 2–3 months of personal expenses + business costs = $12,000–$18,000 (using our example numbers)

Until this reserve is in place, your revenue goal should include an additional savings contribution each month to build it. Once it's built, the reserve absorbs slow months without affecting your ability to pay personal expenses.

This is why the first 12–18 months of focused freelancing often feel harder than the work itself suggests: you're building income *and* building a reserve simultaneously.

## Setting Revenue Goals at Different Freelance Stages

### Early Stage (First 1–2 Years)

Priority: hit break-even. Don't let perfect be the enemy of functional. If your break-even is $6,500/month, your goal is $6,500/month — ideally achieved through 2 to 3 small-to-medium clients at rates that actually cover your costs.

Common mistake at this stage: taking any work at any rate to "get experience." Some below-rate work is fine for portfolio building. But doing it exclusively means you build your business on a foundation of underpricing that's hard to correct later.

### Growth Stage (Years 2–4)

Priority: grow revenue above break-even through rate increases and better client mix. By this stage, you should be replacing your lowest-paying clients annually — with each year's floor higher than the last.

Track your effective hourly rate (total income ÷ total hours worked, including admin). If this isn't growing year-over-year, something is wrong — either your rates aren't increasing or your admin load is growing faster than your billing.

### Established Stage (Years 4+)

Priority: optimize for profit and lifestyle, not just gross revenue. At this point, many freelancers discover that their ideal revenue goal is actually lower than they thought — because they've built systems that reduce wasted time, they've cut low-value clients, and they're earning more per hour with less volume.

Some established freelancers deliberately plateau their revenue in exchange for reduced hours. This is a valid goal. Your revenue goal should reflect what you actually want, not what society tells you ambition looks like.

## Revenue Goal Red Flags

Watch for these signs that your revenue goal may be unrealistic or miscalibrated:

**The math doesn't close.** If your goal requires more billable hours than you have in a week, it's not achievable. The math must work before the goal can work.

**It's based on your best month.** One great month doesn't predict the year. Base annual goals on sustainable averages, not peaks.

**It ignores your cost structure.** A $150K revenue goal is very different for a freelancer with $15K in annual expenses vs. one with $60K in expenses. Know your costs.

**It's disconnected from your pricing.** If hitting your revenue goal requires charging $200/hour but your market research suggests $100/hour is the ceiling for your service in your market, you have a positioning problem that no amount of goal-setting fixes.

**It doesn't account for taxes.** This one causes the most pain. Budget for taxes first, not last.

## Building a Quarterly Review Process

Annual revenue goals are set once and checked once — which is often useless. Instead, review your progress quarterly:

**Q1 Review (March):** Is year-to-date revenue on pace? What was your average monthly revenue? Are you hitting target billable hours?

**Q2 Review (June):** Project the year based on current trajectory. Do you need to accelerate pipeline? Should you raise rates for H2?

**Q3 Review (September):** Identify which months tend to be slow in Q4 (many industries slow in November–December). Build a client plan for Q4.

**Q4 Review (December):** Compare actual vs. target. Calculate what's working and what needs adjustment for next year. Set next year's revenue goal using the same bottom-up framework.

This quarterly cadence turns your revenue goal from a fixed target into a living tool that adjusts to reality.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**What's a realistic first-year freelance revenue goal?**
It depends heavily on your field, experience, and market. For many freelancers, covering personal expenses in year one is the realistic goal — a "break-even year." Year two is when growth targets become more meaningful.

**Should my revenue goal be gross or net?**
Set it as gross revenue (total invoiced), but track both gross and net (after expenses and taxes). Net is what actually funds your life, but gross is what you control directly through pricing and billing.

**How do I set a revenue goal when I don't know how many clients I'll have?**
Work from your current conversion rate and pipeline. If you close 30% of discovery calls and have 2 calls per week, that's roughly 3 new clients per month. Multiply by average project value to estimate monthly gross.

**Should I set a monthly or annual goal?**
Both. Annual for planning, monthly for managing. Convert your annual goal to a monthly average, then track actual against that monthly target. Adjust forward-looking months based on what you've learned.

## The Bottom Line

A freelance revenue goal built on real numbers is one of the most powerful tools you have. It tells you exactly what you need to earn, how many hours you need to bill, and how to price your work to get there.

Stop working toward round numbers and start working toward numbers built from your actual costs, your actual tax situation, and your actual income aspirations. The difference between a $90,000 goal and a $113,000 goal might not be working harder — it might be charging 20% more per project.
