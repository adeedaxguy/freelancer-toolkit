"I want to make six figures this year." It’s one of the most common freelance goals — and one of the least useful ways to frame what you actually want. $100,000 in gross revenue sounds compelling until you subtract taxes, business expenses, and slow months. What’s left might be far less than $100K suggests.

Setting a **freelance revenue goal** that actually works means working backward from what you need and want your life to look like — not forward from a round number. Done right, a revenue goal becomes a daily decision-making tool: which clients to take, what to charge, when to say no.

---

## Why Round Number Goals Fail Freelancers

Most freelancers set revenue goals like New Year’s resolutions: aspirational, round, and disconnected from any logic. $100K. $150K. $200K.

The problems:

**They ignore the tax gap.** A US freelancer earning $100K gross can expect to pay 25–35% in combined taxes. That’s $65K–$75K take-home before business expenses.

**They don’t account for slow months.** A $120K annual goal averages $10K/month — but some months may be $4K and others $18K.

**They don’t connect to actual decisions.** “I want $150K” doesn’t tell you whether to take a $3,000 project or raise your rates.

## The Framework: Build Your Revenue Goal From the Bottom Up

### Step 1: Define Your Personal Income Requirement

Start with your actual monthly expenses:
- Housing, groceries, utilities, transportation
- Health insurance and medical
- Debt payments
- Personal subscriptions and entertainment
- Travel (amortized)
- Savings target (emergency fund + retirement)

The [Bureau of Labor Statistics Consumer Expenditure Survey](https://www.bls.gov/cex/) shows most Americans significantly underestimate their actual monthly spending.

**Example: $5,200/month = $62,400/year**

### Step 2: Add Business Expenses

Software, equipment, professional development, marketing, accounting, insurance.

**Example: $800/month = $9,600/year**

### Step 3: Calculate Your Tax Reserve

Self-employed freelancers pay self-employment tax (15.3% in the US) plus income tax. Reserve 25–30% of net income.

$62,400 × 0.30 = **$18,720/year**

### Step 4: Sum Your Annual Revenue Requirement

| Component | Annual |
|-----------|--------|
| Personal income | $62,400 |
| Business expenses | $9,600 |
| Tax reserve | $18,720 |
| **Minimum Revenue** | **$90,720** |

### Step 5: Add a Profit Margin

Break-even is the floor, not the goal. Add 20–30% above break-even:

$90,720 × 1.25 = **$113,400 annual revenue goal**

Now $100K has meaning: it’s not quite enough to hit your profit target.

## Translating Revenue Goals Into Actionable Metrics

### Monthly Target
$113,400 ÷ 12 = **$9,450/month average** — but plan for volatility:
- High months: $12,000–$15,000
- Average months: $9,000–$11,000
- Slow months (2–3/year): $4,000–$7,000

### Billable Hours Required
At $125/hour: $9,450 ÷ $125 = **75.6 hours/month** (~18 hours/week billable)

### Client Count
At $4,500 average project: $9,450 ÷ $4,500 = **2.1 projects/month**
At $2,500 retainer: $9,450 ÷ $2,500 = **3.8 retainer clients**

## Adjusting for Income Volatility

Build a **cash reserve of 2–3 months of expenses** ($12,000–$18,000 using our example). Until it’s built, include an extra savings contribution in your monthly target. Once built, the reserve absorbs slow months without crisis.

## Revenue Goals at Different Freelance Stages

**Early stage (Years 1–2):** Priority is break-even. Don’t take any work at any rate — underpricing trains clients to expect cheap work and is hard to correct later.

**Growth stage (Years 2–4):** Grow above break-even through rate increases and better client mix. Replace your lowest-paying client annually.

**Established (Years 4+):** Optimize for profit and lifestyle. Many freelancers discover their ideal revenue goal is lower than expected — better systems and higher rates mean more income with less volume.

## Build a Quarterly Review Process

- **Q1 (March):** Is year-to-date revenue on pace? Are billable hours on target?
- **Q2 (June):** Project the year. Accelerate pipeline? Raise rates for H2?
- **Q3 (September):** Plan for Q4 slow months (November–December)
- **Q4 (December):** Actual vs. target. Set next year’s goal.

---

## Revenue Goal Red Flags

- **The math doesn’t close** — required hours exceed available hours
- **Based on your best month** — use sustainable averages, not peaks
- **Ignores taxes** — the most painful mistake
- **Disconnected from pricing** — if your market caps at $100/hour but your goal needs $200/hour, you have a positioning problem

## Frequently Asked Questions

**What’s a realistic first-year freelance revenue goal?**
Covering personal expenses is realistic in year one. Year two is when growth targets become meaningful.

**Should my revenue goal be gross or net?**
Set as gross (total invoiced), track both. Net is what funds your life; gross is what you control through pricing.

**How do I set a goal when I don’t know how many clients I’ll have?**
Work from your conversion rate. If you close 30% of calls and have 2/week, that’s ~3 new clients/month. Multiply by average project value.
