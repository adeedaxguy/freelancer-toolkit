An SEO proposal has one job: make the client feel that the work is specific, commercial, and manageable.

Weak SEO proposals promise "rankings" in broad language. Strong SEO proposals show what is wrong now, what will be done next, how success will be measured, and what the client needs to approve.

## Quick answer

A strong SEO proposal should include:

1. The business problem behind the SEO work
2. A concise audit summary with evidence
3. Clear deliverables and exclusions
4. Timeline and reporting cadence
5. Pricing structure and assumptions
6. One concrete next step to approve

## Start with the business problem, not "SEO services"

Clients do not buy SEO because they want more keywords in a spreadsheet. They buy SEO because they want a business outcome.

Open with a problem like:

- Commercial pages are not visible for high-intent searches
- Organic traffic is growing but not turning into leads
- Competitors own the comparison and category terms
- Technical issues are limiting indexation or crawlability
- Existing content attracts the wrong traffic

That framing changes the proposal from a task list into a business document.

## Include a short audit summary

You do not need to give away the full strategy, but the proposal should still feel researched.

Useful inputs:

- Priority keyword opportunities
- Content gaps
- Technical blockers
- Thin or weak commercial pages
- Competitor advantages
- Reporting or measurement gaps

This section should be specific enough to prove relevance and short enough to keep the sales momentum.

## Define deliverables in plain language

Avoid vague phrases like "ongoing SEO support."

Instead, specify what the client gets:

| Deliverable area | Example |
| --- | --- |
| Technical SEO | Crawl review, prioritized fixes, sitemap and indexing checks |
| On-page SEO | Title, meta, heading, internal-link, and schema updates |
| Content | Briefs, page refreshes, or new support pages |
| Reporting | Monthly dashboard, keyword watchlist, next-step recommendations |
| Strategy | Kickoff call and implementation roadmap |

If implementation is not included, say that clearly.

## Price the real work, not only the visible edits

SEO includes invisible labor:

- Research
- Opportunity scoring
- Competitor review
- QA
- Reporting
- Communication
- Prioritization

That is why pricing only from page-edit time usually underquotes the work.

## Show how progress will be measured

Clients trust SEO proposals more when measurement is visible from the start.

Define:

- Which pages matter first
- Which keyword themes are being attacked
- What counts as progress
- How often reporting happens
- What the client should expect in the first 30 to 60 days

You do not need to promise results on a fixed date. You do need to show a clear measurement path.

## Include assumptions and exclusions

This is where many SEO proposals fail.

Clarify items like:

- Whether implementation is included
- Whether content writing is included
- Whether link building is included
- Whether dev support is required from the client
- Whether GA4, GSC, and CMS access are required

Clear exclusions reduce future pricing disputes.

## End with one approval step

Your close should tell the client what happens next.

Examples:

- Approve the proposal and pay the deposit
- Confirm the selected monthly package
- Book the kickoff call
- Share access to analytics, search console, and CMS

That works better than "let me know what you think."

## Common mistakes in SEO proposals

- Selling generic "SEO services"
- Listing tasks without a business reason
- Giving away too much strategy for free
- Hiding exclusions
- Pricing only the visible edits
- Failing to explain reporting and measurement

## FAQ

### What should an SEO proposal include?

Include the business goal, current search problem, recommended deliverables, timeline, reporting cadence, pricing, assumptions, exclusions, and a clear approval step.

### How long should an SEO proposal be?

Most freelance or small-agency SEO proposals work best at 3 to 6 pages. Put the decision summary first and move deeper notes later.

### Should I include keyword research in an SEO proposal?

Yes, but selectively. Show enough keyword or competitor evidence to justify the plan without turning the proposal into a free full strategy document.
